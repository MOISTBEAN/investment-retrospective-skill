# Market Fear Checklist

Use this checklist to identify what the market was likely worried about before a stock re-rated, collapsed, or recovered. Reconstruct the fear from contemporaneous evidence; do not infer it solely from the later outcome.

## Minimum evidence gate

Do not promote a proposed fear from **candidate** to **documented** unless:

1. At least two contemporaneous indicators point to the same concern.
2. At least one indicator directly connects the concern to a valuation or operating assumption.
3. The evidence is not merely two outlets repeating one original claim.

If the case fails this gate, label it `candidate fear — low confidence`. A price decline by itself never passes the gate.

## Core questions

- [ ] What was the stock punished for?
- [ ] What operating, financial, or valuation assumption was being challenged?
- [ ] Was the fear about revenue, margins, customer concentration, financing, execution, regulation, competition, technology, or valuation?
- [ ] Did management address the concern directly?
- [ ] Did analysts repeatedly ask about the topic?
- [ ] Did reputable headlines cluster around the same concern?
- [ ] Did the stock underperform suitable peers during the fear period?
- [ ] Was the fear later resolved, partially resolved, unresolved, or confirmed?
- [ ] What counter-evidence existed at the time?
- [ ] Is the identified fear a sourced market view or the analyst's inference?

## Common market fear categories

### Customer loss risk

The market fears the company may lose a major customer, key order, program, or share of wallet. Check concentration, customer confirmation, design-cycle timing, and whether an order is recurring.

### Demand slowdown

The market fears end-market demand is weakening. Separate customer inventory correction, cyclical softness, structural decline, and company-specific share loss.

### Margin pressure

The market fears revenue growth will not translate into profit. Check mix, pricing, input costs, utilization, ramp costs, and operating leverage.

### Execution risk

The market fears the company cannot deliver its plan. Look for missed milestones, delayed products, capacity constraints, cost overruns, or repeated guidance changes.

### Technology uncertainty

The market doubts whether the technology works, scales, meets customer requirements, or remains competitive.

### Financing risk

The market fears dilution, refinancing difficulty, debt stress, covenant pressure, or insufficient liquidity.

### Regulatory risk

The market fears regulation, approval delays, export controls, litigation, or other legal constraints.

### Competitive pressure

The market fears competitors will erode pricing, market share, customer relevance, or technological advantage.

### Valuation compression

The market fears expectations embedded in the valuation are falling. Separate weaker fundamentals from a lower multiple caused by rates, risk appetite, or sector rotation.

## Evidence hierarchy

### Stronger evidence

- Earnings-call questions repeatedly focus on the same risk.
- Guidance changes explicitly reference the risk.
- Credible analyst research identifies the risk and affected assumption.
- The stock underperforms relevant peers during the risk period.
- Management repeatedly responds to the risk.
- Customer, regulator, or supplier evidence directly bears on the concern.

### Weaker evidence

- Random social-media discussion
- A one-off headline
- A price decline without a documented topic
- Vague “market concern” language without supporting sources
- Commentary published only after the outcome was known

## Resolution test

Classify the result:

- **Resolved:** Direct evidence materially rejects the feared outcome.
- **Partially resolved:** Evidence reduces the risk, but a key uncertainty remains.
- **Unresolved:** Available evidence does not settle the fear.
- **Confirmed:** Direct evidence supports the feared outcome.
- **Too uncertain:** The fear itself or its outcome lacks adequate evidence.

State what evidence would have distinguished resolution from temporary relief. Do not call a fear resolved merely because the stock recovered.
