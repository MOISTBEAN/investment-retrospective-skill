# Source Quality Checklist

Use this checklist to classify sources and decide how each source may support an investment retrospective. Source quality is multidimensional: classify source tier, evidence proximity, claim type, evidence grade, attribution weight, and allowed usage separately.

**Important rules:**

- Price action can confirm that the market reacted, but it cannot alone prove why the market reacted.
- Management commentary can establish company framing, but usually requires external validation.
- Social media can reveal sentiment or generate research questions, but cannot anchor factual claims.
- A strong source does not turn an inference into a fact. Classify the claim as well as the source.
- Multiple articles repeating the same filing, transcript, or anonymous source count as one evidence origin, not independent confirmation.

## 1. Source Tier

### Tier 1 — Primary / Direct Source

**Definition:** Direct material from the company, regulator, exchange, customer, supplier, or another official party to the event.

**Examples:**

- 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, and equivalent filings
- Earnings releases and call transcripts
- Investor presentations and company press releases
- Exchange filings and regulator documents
- Customer or supplier official announcements

**Allowed usage:**

- Confirm that an event or official statement occurred
- Extract exact financial or operating numbers
- Anchor management commentary
- Confirm guidance, margins, revenue, CapEx, orders, or disclosed partnerships

**Limitations:**

- Management commentary may be promotional, selective, or forward-looking.
- An official source confirms what was disclosed, not necessarily the economic outcome or reason for a market move.
- A transcript quote requires surrounding context.

**Attribution weight:** High for direct factual claims; medium-high for accurately labeled management claims; insufficient alone for market causality.

### Tier 2 — High-quality Secondary Source

**Definition:** Credible financial media or reputable specialist industry publication using identifiable reporting standards.

**Examples:**

- Reuters, Bloomberg, The Wall Street Journal, Financial Times, Nikkei, and CNBC
- The Information
- Reputable industry and specialist research publications

**Allowed usage:**

- Corroborate reported events
- Establish contemporaneous market or industry context
- Document investor concerns
- Supplement primary sources

**Limitations:**

- Separate reported facts from journalist interpretation.
- Treat unnamed-source reporting with appropriate caution.
- Reporting may reflect consensus framing rather than independent validation.

**Attribution weight:** Medium; potentially higher for directly sourced reporting, but still limited for proving market causality.

### Tier 3 — Sell-side / Analyst Source

**Definition:** Broker research, rating actions, estimate changes, target-price changes, or attributable analyst commentary.

**Examples:**

- Upgrades and downgrades
- Target-price or estimate revisions
- Research-note headlines and reports
- Attributable conference commentary

**Allowed usage:**

- Reconstruct market expectations and concerns
- Track changes in valuation frameworks or estimates
- Identify contemporary bull and bear cases
- Detect when professional market narratives begin to change

**Limitations:**

- Analyst changes may lag price action or follow consensus.
- Analyst interpretation is not a primary factual source unless it accurately cites one.
- A target-price change is evidence of an analyst view, not intrinsic value.

**Attribution weight:** Medium for market perception; low-medium for factual claims.

### Tier 4 — Market-derived Evidence

**Definition:** Price, volume, relative return, valuation, options, positioning, or short-interest data.

**Examples:**

- Stock price reaction and abnormal return
- Volume spike
- Short interest or options-implied volatility
- Valuation multiple expansion or compression
- Peer-relative performance

**Allowed usage:**

- Confirm that the market reacted
- Identify event windows
- Support an attribution hypothesis
- Test whether a reaction appears stock-specific or sector-driven

**Limitations:**

- Price confirms reaction, not cause.
- Compare with an appropriate index, sector, peers, and macro backdrop.
- Account for overlapping news, market hours, liquidity, positioning, and return-window choices.

**Attribution weight:** Medium as reaction evidence; low as standalone causal evidence.

### Tier 5 — Social / Community / Unverified Commentary

**Definition:** Social media, forums, unsourced commentary, screenshots, or rumors.

**Examples:**

- X / Twitter, Reddit, Stocktwits, YouTube commentary
- Discord or Telegram screenshots
- Forums and unsourced rumors

**Allowed usage:**

- Identify popular fears or sentiment
- Observe how a narrative spread
- Generate hypotheses for verification

**Limitations:**

- Do not anchor factual claims or primary attribution.
- Treat screenshots and reposts as unverified until traced to the original source.
- Label low confidence unless independently verified.

**Attribution weight:** Low.

## 2. Evidence Proximity

### Direct

The source directly supports the claim. Example: a filing states that revenue guidance increased.

### Near-direct

The source is close to the event but not official. Example: a reputable industry publication reports named channel checks indicating demand improvement.

### Indirect

The source supports context rather than the claim itself. Example: peers report stronger demand, which may indicate a sector trend but does not prove company-specific improvement.

### Speculative

The source relies on rumor, inference, or unverified commentary. Example: a social post asserts a customer win without evidence.

## 3. Claim Type

Classify the claim itself, not merely its source:

- **reported_fact:** A concrete event happened.
- **reported_number:** A specific number was reported.
- **management_claim:** Management stated something about demand, customers, strategy, margins, or outlook.
- **customer_validation:** A customer, partner, regulator, or external party validates the company's role or milestone.
- **analyst_interpretation:** An analyst explains what an event may mean.
- **market_reaction:** Verified price, volume, valuation, options, or positioning behavior.
- **price_signal:** Price behavior suggests possible repricing; this remains interpretive.
- **rumor:** An unverified claim.
- **inference:** A reasoned interpretation based on identified evidence.
- **attribution_hypothesis:** A possible explanation for a market move.

## 4. Evidence Grade

Grade the support for the specific claim, not the publisher's reputation in isolation.

Apply these caps:

- Speculative proximity cannot exceed Grade D.
- Indirect proximity cannot exceed Grade C for a company-specific factual claim.
- A management claim without independent validation cannot exceed Grade B for the underlying economic conclusion.
- Market-derived evidence cannot exceed Grade C for causal attribution, regardless of data quality.

### Grade A

Direct, specific, timely, and independently verifiable. Examples include a filing with exact numbers, an official customer announcement, or a contextualized transcript statement.

### Grade B

Credible but partly interpretive or not independently confirmed. Examples include named-source reputable reporting, an analyst note grounded in reported numbers, or a specific management response to a market fear.

### Grade C

Indirect or contextual. Examples include peer trends, broad industry data, or analyst commentary without specific new evidence.

### Grade D

Speculative, vague, or low-confidence. Examples include unsourced social claims, anonymous rumors, or market chatter.

## 5. Attribution Weight and Confidence

### High-confidence attribution

Require most of the following:

- The event directly relates to the original market fear.
- Tier 1 or strong Tier 2 evidence supports the event and its interpretation.
- A clear abnormal return exists versus appropriate peers or index.
- The event changes a forward-looking assumption.
- Contemporaneous commentary links the event and repricing.
- Follow-up evidence reinforces the same narrative.
- Alternative explanations were tested and are materially weaker.

High confidence should be uncommon. Direct confirmation that market participants acted for one reason is rarely available.

### Medium-confidence attribution

Usually includes a relevant event, meaningful reaction, credible supporting evidence, partial follow-through, and at least one plausible alternative explanation.

### Low-confidence attribution

Usually relies on timing correlation, weak or speculative evidence, little abnormal return, sector-driven movement, or no follow-up validation.

## 7. Source Review Checks

- [ ] Open and read the original source rather than relying on a search snippet or repost.
- [ ] Record title, publisher, author or issuing entity, publication date, and URL or document identifier.
- [ ] Distinguish event date from publication date.
- [ ] Confirm the source predates any conclusion claimed to be knowable at the time.
- [ ] Capture enough context to avoid quote mining.
- [ ] Trace reported numbers to the primary source where practical.
- [ ] Note corrections, updates, paywall limits, or inaccessible material.
- [ ] Avoid double-counting multiple articles that repeat one original source.
- [ ] Identify conflicts and explain which source receives more weight and why.
- [ ] Group repeated coverage by its original evidence origin before judging confirmation.
